Reflection: Improving a content management system

May-June 2020

Recently I ran a user research project at work which aimed to validate the assumptions the Product Team had on how to improve a content management system (CMS) for regulatory content. The project was divided into two parts, firstly to find out where users got the most value out of the current CMS and secondly to understand their step by step process for monitoring, managing, communicating and reviewing change in their organisation. I worked alongside a Product Marketing colleague to complete this project. In total, we interviewed 17 customers.

The research findings aided the Product Team to develop a set of requirements which reflected their best understanding about what the users needed in a CMS. The findings, and subsequent requirements, were used by the Research & Innovation (R&I) team to identify the possibility of adding features that take advantage of machine learning and to develop a proof of concept (PoC) for what the CMS could look like.

This case study outlines my research process, the challenges I faced, the successes and areas of improvement.

Step 1: Planning the research

To plan the research project I engaged the Product Manager and stakeholders early, and regularly, in the planning process. I got regular feedback on the discussion guide, ensuring it met their expectations and covered all topic areas. This helped me avoid potentially missing key pieces of information when conducting the interviews. The online whiteboarding tool, Miro, helped me achieve this because of its flexibility to brainstorm, collaborative nature, ease of leaving comments, and its accessibility within the company. This made it easy for stakeholders to give their input and leave feedback.

Due to the success of this approach, I will continue to engage stakeholders in the planning stages of research. Regular communication and feedback with them gave me the confidence that I was meeting their expectations, and our understanding was aligned. From their perspective, it ensured I was on track with the work and understood their aims and needs. Subsequently, I believe this positive working relationship between us in the planning process ensured I delivered the expected outputs and avoided any miscommunication and misunderstandings.

Originally, research sessions were organised via long email chains. This was not a streamlined process and was time-consuming and annoying for the participant and I. To organise the interviews for this project, I used the scheduling tool Calendly. For me, making the sign-up process quick and easy for participants was important for decreasing the number of potential drop-offs. Since using Calendly, I have significantly improved my process for organising research sessions. As a next step, I need to share this tool with my team, so we have a shared process when organising research.

Despite Covid-19, we planned to conduct remote interviews because it was quicker and cheaper than in-person interviews. Also, we were interviewing users to understand their process rather than observing their behaviour.

Step 2: Recruiting participants

Recruiting the participants was the most challenging stage of this project. The user research culture within the organisation was not well established and the companies target audience was niche. This made it difficult to recruit enough participants who met the desired characteristics. As a result, my colleague worked with the Marketing team in both parts of the project, to collate a list of potential customers to contact and invite to participate. I felt this was the best approach for several reasons. Firstly, my colleague knew the process of sending out communications and had access to the customer database. Secondly, Marketing could help us make sure we met GDPR regulations and only contact customers who had consented to hear from us. However, their method of convenience sampling was problematic for me.

The most significant issue of this sampling and recruitment approach was the bias it created within our sample. Due to their previous communications with the company, the selected contacts appeared to all be engaged with the organisation, product and wanted to help. Therefore, they were not representative of the entire customer base. This meant we did not have a range of alternative perspectives and experiences. This experience taught me the importance of having a representative and adequately sized target audience to draw participants from. To avoid this issue in the future, I will employ a sampling methods such as systematic or stratified, to ensure a representative sample is drawn from the target audience.

For the second part of the project, the goal was to speak to 5 participants. More would have been better, but we decided to set a realistic and achievable number due to limited access to potential candidates, as previously described. Only one customer responded to the initial research invite. This felt disappointing as in the first round of research numerous customers responded to our invite quickly.

To overcome this situation, I took the initiative to engage the Customer Success team to recruit participants. As a result of working with the team, 13 interviews were arranged in total (many more than the original plan of 5!). I involved the Customer Success team for various reasons. Firstly, as with the Marketing team, they are in a customer-facing role and speak to customers every day. This placed them in an ideal position to promote the research. Secondly, I wanted to avoid duplicated effort across the business, prevent siloed working and make the business look cohesive from a customer perspective. This experience showed me how difficult it can be to contact customers. In the future, I will engage this team earlier in the recruitment process to utilise their contacts and resources to promote the research. It also demonstrated how UX and user research could work with other customer-facing departments such as Sales, Customer Support and the Implementations team. As a next step, my manager, team and I should aim to increase the businesses awareness of user research, its purpose and how others can work with us to perform it.

One key learning from this challenging situation was the importance of keeping the stakeholder informed. I learnt that positive stakeholder relationships are not only built on "good" events but the challenges too. It builds a relationship on trust and confidence, shows you are a responsible individual, can take ownership of your work and have the initiative to overcome problems. These are all skills that management and stakeholders look for, making it more likely that new projects and opportunities will open up for you.

Step 3: Conducting the research

I purposefully organised the interviews to be spread out over a couple of weeks. In previous projects, I aimed to get the interviews completed as quickly as possible. Although this is required in some scenarios, for example, when there are tight deadlines. It is not always optimal due to interview fatigue. I found conducting the interviews and doing the analysis in a repetitive cycle improved my ability to digest and analyse the findings. Avoiding a linear process meant I was not overwhelmed with information and did not experience tiredness and lack of concentration after completing all the interviews. It also gave me time to reflect on the findings and formulate themes and differences between each interview. I found this approach effective and will aim to repeat it in the future. In situations where circumstances require a more linear approach, I will manage my time and allow myself and others involved, to reflect on each research session even if it means not doing a full analysis of it.

Due to the nature of the business enterprise, some of the participants I spoke to were dissatisfied with the current version of their product. To begin with, I found this challenging because I felt unsure about how to respond to their queries and comments, manage their expectations and provide solutions to their problems. However, as I performed more interviews, I became more confident and sure of my responses. My main takeaway was to answer their questions as best you can, but be honest with the participant/customer, tell them when you don't know the answer, ask for input from another colleague or put them in contact with the appropriate member of staff. This proactive approach will provide satisfaction, make them feel listened to and initiate a solution to their problem. To deal with situations where the participant spoke off-topic, I used my discussion guide to steer the conversation back on track. I found this useful because it helped me do so in a considerate way and ensured I got what I needed from the research. Learning how to keep a balance between satisfying the participant and letting them express their thoughts as a paying customer, whilst achieving my own goals, was an interesting learning.

To maintain stakeholder engagement, I invited the Product Manager to the first interview. Listening to the participant first hand, helps them to truly understand their user's needs and dislodge any assumptions they have. This will help them make reliable product decisions. Also, stakeholder buy adds credibility to your work when putting the findings and recommendations into practice. I found their attendance useful because they gave feedback and identified areas of improvement. I also noted how they concisely described and explained topics and product features, so I could reuse the dialogue in the following interviews. This helped me develop my interviewing technique and style which made the sessions run more smoothly.

The key points I will take from these experiences and aim to implement in future research projects are: spread the research sessions out to avoid fatigue and make time for analysis; understand what methods the company has for resolving customer issues; always have a discussion guide on hand to keep the conversation on track and invite the stakeholder to the research to improve yourself, gain credibility and improve their understanding of the user.

Step 4: Presenting and disseminating findings

Due to annual leave I was not involved in the analysis process. As a result, my colleague took the lead to present the research findings and recommendations to the stakeholders. After the presentation, colleagues in others of the business were interested in our research, this time I presented the findings. At the start, I felt nervous in case I could not answer their questions or explain things properly. These worries were unnecessary as I understood the subject matter and didn't experience any problems. Confidently presenting research findings is an important skill. If findings are not communicated to decision-makers, they will not have any impact. My one takeaway from this experience is to invest time and effort into gaining adequate subject knowledge and practice speaking about different subjects, as this will improve my presentation skills and confidence.

Repeating the research presentation to other business areas highlighted inefficiencies within the company because time and effort was duplicated. In the future, the dissemination of research could be improved by having a central research repository in the company. Having learned a lot about research repositories from the ResearchOps community, I believed this was something the company would benefit from. For example, helping to build a research culture, saving time and effort and encouraging collaborative working.

Conclusion

This project aimed to validate the assumptions the Product Team had on how to improve a CMS for the regulatory content. This was achieved by performing 15 interviews with customers and presenting the findings and recommendations to the Product Team. By carrying out this research, the Product Manager formulated a set of requirements that reflected their best understanding of what the users needed in a CMS. The findings, recommendations and subsequent requirements were used by the R&I team to develop a PoC of what the CMS could look like and its machine learning capabilities.

Outlined below are some of my key learnings that I will aim to implement in future research projects, and in some cases improve upon.

  • Engage stakeholders at all stages of the research to get feedback and approval, increase your credibility and their understanding of the user
  • Be honest with stakeholders, especially when things don't go to plan, this will build a trusting relationship
  • Use systematic or stratified sampling, if appropriate, to get a representative sample with a range of perspectives and experiences
  • Utilise the resources of other business areas to make participant recruitment easier
  • Spread out research sessions so you have time for analysis and avoid fatigue
  • If the participants are paying customers, understand what methods are in place to manage issues to diffuse situations and initiate a solution
  • Always have a discussion guide to keep the research session on track and make sure you achieve your goals
  • If you feel unconfident about something, invest time to learn and practice speaking about it to improve your presentation skills
  • Store findings in a central location in the business to avoid duplicated time and effort in presentations and help to share information and knowledge across departments
  • The journey to becoming a user researcher is never complete, every experience should be used to continuously develop and improve
