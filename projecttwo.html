Case study: User research, communication and developing product features

December 2020

At work I was recently tasked with designing a filter, sort and search system to hand off to the Development team to implement in one of the organisation's products. The project had been started by a colleague a few months prior and had been reallocated to me due to changes in workload. My involvement facilitated the progression of the project as a result of conducting user research. My one key learning was the importance of communication, especially between design and development teams.

This case study outlines what went well, the challenges I faced, how I overcame them and the impact my actions had on the project. The following sections will be discussed:

  1. Speak up if you don't think something is right
  2. Build a relationship with stakeholders
  3. Know your participants
  4. Manage participant expectations
  5. Use Miro to collaborate
  6. Use user research to validate requirements
  7. Store findings in a central research repository
  8. Communicate with developers

1. Speak up if you don't think something is right

The problem with the initial design was that the proposed dropdown components didn't accommodate for the dependencies between the multiple filter criteria. I couldn't understand how the design and its behaviours handled the filtering system. I was sure this was a contributing factor to the project's lack of progress.

To resolve this issue, I took the initiative to research alternative types of filter systems. Based on the given filter requirements I produced low fidelity wireframe using a tag cloud and mapped out the filter dependencies using a matrix. I proposed my ideas to the Product Managers (PMs) and explained why I thought this was a better alternative to the initial designs. They agreed my approach was clearer and approved the work to be taken forward to development.

From this experience, I learnt the importance of speaking up when something feels wrong. My new design and behaviours resolved confusion which meant the project could progress from the deadlock it had been in. I could start performing user research and the developers could explore the backend behaviour.

Making your voice heard, at the appropriate time, will facilitate discussion and ideation. It can challenge traditional ways of thinking and processes, encourages innovation, inspires others and provides trust and leadership. If you are open and honest it will encourage others to give feedback and share their perspectives. Doing this respectfully and consistently will turn you into a person that people turn to when they want honest and constructive feedback. This will allow you to exercise your influence, drive the direction of projects and open unexpected opportunities for yourself.

2. Build a relationship with the stakeholders

The main benefit of engaging stakeholders in this project was their approval of, and consequent involvement in, user research. Historically in the company, design and development time has been hand to mouth, designing one week and developing the next. The company culture meant user research and testing wasn't seen as integral to the design and development life cycle. In addition, UX was not seen as a customer-facing role which meant there weren't any processes in place to conduct research. Despite these challenges and due to the complex nature of the feature, I proposed to the PMs that we perform user research. Fortunately, they agreed.

Their approval can be attributed to several things. Firstly, as stakeholders I built a good working relationship with them by continuously presenting work and ideas with evidence and reasoning, updating them on progress, challenging ideas and decisions and asking for feedback. As a result, the confidence and trust they had in me grew, which meant I could push boundaries, challenge processes and the mindset of "That's just the way things are done".

Secondly, I started small and aimed to try something new to see a small incremental change. Little projects such as this, although not always perfect, can be used as evidence when making lasting change. For example, ideally, this user research would have been completed at the start, and continuously throughout, the project, not once halfway through. In an ideal world, I would have liked to speak to more than 3 customers. Nevertheless, the fact I managed to speak to anyone demonstrated a noticeable shift in attitude and understanding about the role of research in developing software and that I had begun to break down the historical organisational boundaries. Small wins such as these are valuable because they add up to create the bigger picture of lasting change. In my case making user research and testing a mandatory part of the design and development life cycle.

The impact of conducting user research on the project was obvious. We now understood our user's needs better. This meant I could work with the PMs to clearly, and more easily, define the requirements and align understanding between Product, UX and Development about what we were trying to achieve.

3. Know your participants

The organisation's products are customisable and each customer has different features in their product across different versions. I felt apprehensive before speaking to each customer because I did not have all the knowledge about their particular product and couldn't tailor the questions to them specifically.

To overcome this I asked open-ended discussion points such as "Tell me about...", "Can you explain how...". Presenting questions like this allows you to subtly build a picture of the participant without them knowing. I also asked them to share their screen so I could visibly see what product they had. Doing this allows you to ask questions about specific features on the screen. Once understanding is gained, and the participant feels comfortable, the conversation flows naturally.

Setting up a customer database, where key information about each customer is stored and easily accessible to anyone in the company, would be beneficial for user research in the future. A tool such as this would prepare user researchers in advance of their sessions, encourage collaboration and the sharing of knowledge between customer-facing roles, and make the company look more professional and coherent from a customer perspective.

4. Manage participant expectations

During one interview, I felt some tension between the customer and the organisation when the participant realised they would not get the feature we were discussing unless they paid to upgrade their product.

Researching customers with existing products can be more difficult than research on the general public. Customers expect a level of knowledge and preparation, they want to get something out of taking part and understand how they will benefit. In comparison, the general population is unlikely to have expectations, will take part out of self-interest or chance (depending on the sample and research method) and will accept more ambiguity about the nature of the research, compared to paying customers.

To avoid situations such as these it is important to manage the participant's expectations. Communicate to the participant at the invite stage what the nature and purpose of the research is, allow open dialogue between the participant and yourself, invite them to ask you questions before the research is completed, and be transparent about the use of the research. Addressing these potential issues early will reduce misunderstanding later.

5. Use Miro to collaborate

To analyse the findings, I worked remotely with a junior colleague. We used Miro to create an affinity map then categorised and themed the findings. After this, I formulated solutions for each finding and prioritised them into short term and long term features.

Miro allows you to collaborate with others, on the same thing at the same time whilst showing real-time changes. Paired with a video call, you can complete your work more quickly because you can discuss, share ideas and thoughts in real-time. You don't need to wait for someone to upload their work and wait for communication so they can explain what they have done. Also, you know you are always working on the latest version of something.

Compared to other products, such as Trello, Miro makes it easy to organise ideas and collect notes. You are not constrained to a layout or a specific order and this encourages you to work flexibly and freely. In addition, Miro boards are infinite, so all work around one project can be stored and updated in one place, rather than having multiple files that become out of sync with each other and lost.

Miro's user experience is excellent. Its clean user interface and intuitive navigation make it easy to use, even for those who have not used it before, such as my junior colleague. This is useful because they can onboard themselves onto the tool, unlike other tools I have worked with such Office 365.

6. Use user research to validate requirements

The user research findings were used to clarify and prioritise the requirements and create a shared understanding between the teams. I did this by identifying what the user needs were, based on their organisational setup and how they worked. Compared to the start of the project, we now had a clear structure and a timeline to implement the requirements. This made discussions easier because they were more focused, which helped progress the project.

Having a clear set of requirements that meet user needs is important for many reasons. For instance, they define the purpose of the feature and the criteria for the minimum viable product. They allow the PM, and the teams involved, to monitor changes in requirements and track their progress at any point of the product lifecycle. This helps the project team monitor the project's overall progress and plan resources.

If the user needs are not understood in the early stages, it risks problems later on. The time and cost spent to rectify these problems may be detrimental to the business. Lastly, working out the product requirements early on gives teams a clear goal, aligns understanding and allows time for focused exploration and ideation.

7. Store findings in a central repository

I recorded the findings and requirements timeline in Miro and in Figma, where the designs were stored. I annotated the designs and identified which features were for the first and future releases. Despite recording this research, I was concerned about its accessibility and whether it would be remembered 6 months, a year down the line. I knew this issue would grow as more research was conducted. As a result, I told my manager about the benefits of a research repository and highlighted my concerns. Finding an appropriate repository and its implementation is still being explored.

To grow a research culture, I think it is important that organisations have a central repository to store data. This is so it can be accessed, shared and used throughout the organisation. Having all the research insights, recommendations and product roadmaps in one place makes it easier to organise and plan projects across teams. In addition, with the correct features, a repository helps to manage data by organising it and ensuring it is accurate. This helps management teams make effective decisions based on accurate and reliable data. Also, if the data is managed in one place, changes and updates are reflected automatically across whole workflows, rather than relying on someone to update individual systems. Ultimately, organisations with a research repository will generate more revenue and save more money than companies who do not, due to better quality data and informed decision making.

8. Communicate with developers

When I presented the new designs and behaviours to the developers, I experienced push back on the requirements. The developers explained the service architecture underneath my designs, and the proposed requirements, did not exist and would take a large amount of time and effort to build. They argued the resource needed to build it outweighed how essential it was to the overall product roadmap. They basically said, "We can't build that filter system based on the criteria you have given us, can we just not include it?".

When discussing the requirements, I assumed the PMs were aware of the underlying architecture and had an understanding of what could be built. This experience highlighted the importance of not making assumptions and communicating with developers early and often. After discussion between the PMs and Development, I changed my designs based on what was achievable in the back end architecture. Compromises were made to the user experience, however, the PMs weighed up the cost and benefits and were willing to make this trade-off. Once we were all aligned, the Development team began to investigate and build the back end.

Although UX design does not want to be driven by development, you need to be on the same page. This ensures you have a shared understanding at a high level about what is possible. Designs should be shown to developers early and often to get feedback on finer details such as component behaviour, user flows and interactions. Frequent communication prevents miscommunication and will ultimately lead to better developed designs. It will also avoid delays and redesign in the later stages of the project, which can cause frustration amongst everyone involved.

The best project I have worked on followed the above approaches. Its success was recognised at all levels of the company, including senior management and the C-Suite. Knowing which developer was implementing my work, so they could be involved in the design stage and I could be involved in the development stage was key to its success. This was achieved through direct contact, clarifying work, asking questions and doing mini demos mid-sprint. This allowed us to bounce ideas off each other and validate concepts before time was invested in them. Conflict, tensions and re-work were avoided helping with the projects successful completion.

Conclusion

Performing user research facilitated the progression of this project. It was fundamental for understanding user needs, validating requirements and aligning understanding between teams. It allowed the PMs to outline the project goal which reduced uncertainty and bought the project into focus. The aligned understanding made discussion and collaboration easier and more constructive. Finally, it validated and rejected our assumptions, meaning we felt confident that we were building something that met the user's needs.

Getting approval to conduct user research signified the break down of traditional organisational barriers that prevented the UX team from performing user research. Its impact in this project will support cultural change in the organisation about attitudes towards, and understanding of, user research.

For me the key takeaways for future projects will be:

  • Better communication with developers to ensure there is a clear understanding between design and development teams about what is feasible.
  • Conduct user research earlier and more frequently to ensure design and development time is not spent creating a product/feature which is not suitable for the user.
